## Forget About Prompts

ChatGPT trained you to write longer, more detailed prompts. Forget that.

With Cowork, the game is text files. You take everything you know: your writing style, your company's rules, your best examples, your previous work, and you put it into text files. You drop them in a folder. You point Claude to that folder.

It's like having a procedures manual for an employee. But here Claude is your employee, and the manual is your Claude Cowork folder.

The more context you give it as files, the fewer prompts you need. The result goes from "generic AI" to "this sounds like a real employee."

## Step 1: Build Your Folder

Create a dedicated Cowork folder on your computer. It needs a clean, intentional space. This is the recommended structure: 📁 CLAUDE COWORK/

├── 📁 ABOUT ME/

│ ├── about-me.md

│ └── anti-ai-writing-style.md

├── 📁 PROJECTS/

│ └── \[one subfolder for each active project\]

├── 📁 TEMPLATES/

│ └── \[structures of work you want to reuse\]

└── 📁 CLAUDE OUTPUTS/

└── \[this is where Claude will deliver all finished work\]

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HHtPKxjakAA_TcW?format=jpg&name=large)

What is each folder for?

**ABOUT ME** → Who you are, what you do, and how you write. The core of your identity for Claude.

**PROJECTS** → Work in progress. Briefings, drafts, and reference material for each active project, each in its own subfolder.

**TEMPLATES** → Finished work so good that you want to reuse it as a pattern. Not the content itself, but the perfect structure.

**CLAUDE OUTPUTS** → The only place where Claude delivers finished work. Keep this separate and organized.

⚠️ **Important:** Cowork has real read and write access to the folder you share. If something goes wrong, you want the damage to be contained. Keep the folder tight and well-organized.

## Step 2: Create Your Core Files

Inside the ABOUT ME folder, you need to create two key files. They are the foundation of everything.

What is an .md file?

An .md file is simply a plain text file with the .md (Markdown) extension. Claude reads them better than .txt or .docx files. To create one:

1. Open any text editor (Notepad, TextEdit, VS Code, even a Google Doc).
2. Write your content.
3. Save the file as about-me.md instead of about-me.txt.

Done. A good .md file is worth more than 50 random uploads.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HHtPcyxbUAEdLL8?format=jpg&name=large)

Be intentional with what you include.

Complete Template: about-me.md

This file tells Claude who you are, what you do, and what's important to you right now. The more specific, the better.

```markdown
# About Me

## Who I am
[Describe your professional role in 2-3 sentences. Example: "I'm a digital marketing consultant specialized in B2B SaaS. I work with early-stage startups looking to scale their online presence."]

## What I do
[Explain your concrete work. Example: "I create content strategies, manage social media communities, and develop email marketing campaigns for English-speaking clients."]

## My current priorities
[List 3-5 things you're working on right now. Example:]
- Launch a weekly newsletter on AI applied to work
- Create an automation guide for small businesses
- Improve email open rates for my clients

## My main audiences
[Describe who you're targeting. Example: "English-speaking professionals aged 25-40, startup founders, marketing directors, and independent consultants."]

## My communication tone
[Describe how you speak and write. Example: "Direct, no fluff. I prefer short sentences. I use concrete examples. I avoid corporate language. I like subtle humor when it fits."]

## Writing example that represents me
[Paste here a paragraph or fragment of something you've written and are proud of. The more representative of your real style, the better.]

## What I DON'T want
[Explicit list of things Claude should avoid in your work. Example:]
- Long introductions before getting to the point
- Clichés like "In today's world..." or "It's essential that..."
- Conclusions that repeat everything already said
- Excess of empty adjectives and superlatives
```

**Complete Template: anti-ai-writing-style.md**

This is the most important file to make the output not sound like generic AI. Here you define exactly what you want to avoid and what you want to achieve.

```markdown
# Anti-AI Writing Guide

## The problem with AI writing

AI tends to write in a recognizable, generic, and boring way. This file exists to prevent that.

---

## What should NEVER appear in my texts

### Forbidden words and phrases
- "In today's world..."
- "It's essential that..."
- "Without a doubt..."
- "In conclusion..."
- "There's no question that..."
- "Over the years..."
- "Increasingly..."
- "In this sense..."
- "Nowadays..."
- "It's worth mentioning that..."

### Forbidden structures
- Introductions that explain what you're going to say instead of saying it directly
- Conclusions that repeat the main points already mentioned
- Lists of 3 perfectly balanced elements (AI loves them, humans ignore them)
- Paragraphs that end with a rhetorical question to "reflect on"
- Excessive use of exclamation marks to convey enthusiasm

### Style vices
- Stacked adjectives without criteria
- Passive voice when active works better
- Sentences over 25 words when the same can be said in 10
- Euphemisms where the direct word is better

---

## What I DO want in my texts

### Style
- Short sentences. Period. That simple.
- Action verbs. Avoid the verb "to be" when you can use a more concrete one.
- Direct opening: the first paragraph already has to say something interesting.
- Specific examples instead of generalities.

### Structure
- Most important information first (inverted pyramid).
- Subtitles that are statements, not neutral labels.
  - ❌ "Advantages of the method"
  - ✅ "Why this method works when others fail"
- Natural transitions between ideas, not artificial connection formulas.

### Tone
[Adapt this section to your personal tone. Examples:]
- Conversational but professional: like a chat between intelligent colleagues.
- No condescension: treat the reader as someone capable of understanding complex ideas.
- With your own criteria: have an opinion, don't just inform.

---

## Quick quality test

Before delivering any text, ask yourself these questions:
1. Could ChatGPT have written this without any additional context? If yes → rewrite.
2. Does the first paragraph already say something useful? If not → delete the start and begin from there.
3. Is there any sentence that sounds like a "template"? If yes → replace it with something specific.
```

💡 **Practical tip:** Don't try to fill every section perfectly from day one. Start with the essentials (who you are, your tone, the words you hate) and enrich it over time.

## Step 3: Configure Global Instructions

Go to: **Settings → Cowork → Edit Global Instructions**.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HHtQMBXasAAJnEr?format=jpg&name=large)

Global Instructions are like a persistent prompt that Claude reads before every task. You configure it once and it always works.

Copy and paste this exactly:

```markdown
# GLOBAL INSTRUCTIONS

## BEFORE EVERY TASK

1. Read \`ABOUT ME/\`. No task starts without reading both files.
2. If the task relates to a project, read everything in the corresponding \`PROJECTS/\` subfolder before proceeding.
3. If the task involves a type of content that has a pattern in \`TEMPLATES/\`, study that structure first. Use it as a pattern, don't copy the content.

## FOLDER PROTOCOL

You have three read-only folders and one write folder.

### Read-only — never create, edit, or delete anything here:
- \`ABOUT ME/\` → Identity and writing rules.
- \`TEMPLATES/\` → Proven structures to reuse as patterns.
- \`PROJECTS/\` → Briefings, references, and finished work organized by project.

### Write folder — the only place where you deliver work:
- \`CLAUDE OUTPUTS/\` → Everything you create goes here. Organize with one subfolder per project, mirroring the structure of \`PROJECTS/\`. Create the subfolder if it doesn't exist.

## NAMING CONVENTION

All files you create must follow this format:

\`project_content-type_v1.ext\`

Content types: Newsletter, Post, Brief, Deck, Report.

Examples:
- \`AI-Weekly_Newsletter_v1.md\`
- \`ClientX_Report_v2.docx\`
- \`Strategy2026_Deck_v1.pptx\`

Increment the version number if a file with the same name already exists.

## OPERATING RULES

- If the briefing is unclear or incomplete, use the \`AskUserQuestion\` tool. Don't fill the gaps with generic content.
- Don't over-explain. Deliver the work. Save the comments unless asked.
- Never delete files anywhere.
```

You configure it once. It always works. You never write it again.

Combined with your context files, this means your prompts can be 10 words long and still produce work that sounds exactly like you.

## How to Open and Use Cowork

Once you have the folder ready:

1. Open the Claude desktop app (download it at [claude.com/download](https://claude.com/download)).
2. Click the **Cowork** tab at the top.
3. Click **Add Folder** and select your CLAUDE COWORK folder.
4. Make sure to always select **Opus 4.6 and Extended Thinking**. (For more complex tasks you can use 4.7, but it consumes 7x tokens)
5. Type your task. That simple.

## The Feature That Changes Everything: AskUserQuestion

This is the feature that most transforms the way you work. And it's the one most guides explain incorrectly.

Always add this to your prompts:

> "Start by using the AskUserQuestion tool before responding to gather enough context."

When you do this, Cowork generates an interactive form. Real buttons. Selectable options. Multiple options. Rankings you can reorder by dragging.

The AI is asking you the questions. So it can give you the right answer.

## The Prompt That Works for (Almost) Everything

80% of Cowork sessions can start with this same structure:

```text
I want [TASK] for [SUCCESS CRITERIA].

First, explore my CLAUDE COWORK folder. Then, ask me questions using the AskUserQuestion tool. I want to refine the approach with you before you execute.
```

What happens next:

1. Claude reads your context files.
2. Generates a clickable form asking about your audience, goals, and preferences.
3. You click the answers in less than a minute.
4. Claude shows a plan. You approve it.
5. It executes: creates real files in your folder.

If something doesn't add up mid-process, you interrupt it. Claude recalibrates with a new form and resumes from where it left off.

The whole process feels like directing someone competent, not fighting with a text box.

## Plugins: Specialized Skills for Cowork

Anthropic launched 11 official plugins for Cowork: Sales, Marketing, Legal, Finance, Data Analysis, Product Management, Customer Support…

Each one gives Claude specific skills, workflows, and slash commands for that function.

How to install a plugin

1. Open Cowork.
2. Click **Customize** in the left sidebar → Browse plugins.
3. Choose one that fits your work. Click **Install**. It's free.
4. Type / in the chat to see available commands.

You can also customize each plugin for your context: Claude will ask you questions about your company or project and remember the answers.

What it looks like in practice

**Marketing Plugin**

```text
/marketing:draft-content → "Write a LinkedIn post about [topic]. 
Use my voice profile. Target [audience]."
```

What happens: Claude reads your about-me.md, drafts a post that sounds like you, and suggests hook variations. You pick one, edit, publish. Five minutes instead of thirty.

**Data Plugin**

```text
/data:explore → Drop a CSV in the folder.
```

What happens: Claude summarizes each column, flags anomalies, suggests analyses, and can build an interactive dashboard. Writes SQL in natural language. Without touching a formula.

**Legal Plugin**

```text
"Review the NDA in this folder. Flag any unusual or one-sided clauses."
```

What happens: Claude reads the contract, highlights risk clauses, explains them in plain language, and suggests alternative wording. This is what made the legal sector lose $285 billion on the stock market.

## How to Use Cowork Day-to-Day: 3 Real Use Cases

Case 1: Content Writing (newsletter, posts, articles)

**Setup:** The folder has the about-me.md, the anti-ai-writing-style.md, examples of content that has worked well, and reference documentation on the topic to be addressed.

**Prompt:**

```text
I want to write my next newsletter about how to use Gemini to grow on LinkedIn with infographics.

First, explore my CLAUDE COWORK folder. Then ask me questions with AskUserQuestion. I want to refine the approach before you execute.
```

**What happens:** Claude reads each file. Generates a form asking about audience, tone, length, and angles others haven't covered. You answer with clicks. Produces an outline. You can challenge the weak sections. Adjusts. Writes. And because it has the voice profile and the anti-AI guide, the result sounds like a real person.

Case 2: Consulting Deliverables

**Setup:** The client sends a briefing. You drop it in the folder along with your templates and examples of previous work.

**Prompt:**

```text
A client just sent a briefing for a 2026 AI adoption strategy. 
The briefing is in /projects/client-x/.

Read the briefing, my deliverable template, and my previous examples. 
Create a first draft as .docx. Ask me questions first (AskUserQuestion).
```

**What happens:** Claude reads the briefing. Compares it with the template format. Then asks you things you hadn't thought of: "Should I include a calendar or just recommendations?" or "Do you want competitor examples or do we keep the focus internal?" You answer. Creates a .docx directly in your folder.

Case 3: Automated Weekly Briefing

This case is different: Cowork works without you being present.

**Setup:** You create a folder called /weekly-briefings/.

**Prompt (with the /schedule plugin):**

```text
Every Monday at 8:00 AM, generate a weekly briefing. 
Read the latest news on [topics]. 
Summarize in 5 key points. 
Save as Briefing_[date]_v1.md in /weekly-briefings/.
```

**What happens:** Cowork runs automatically every Monday, as long as your computer is on and the app is open. You wake up to a briefing document ready to read.

The Pattern That Repeats in All Cases

In all four use cases, the logic is always the same:

1. Short, direct task — not a long prompt.
2. Point to the folder with the relevant context.
3. Ask it to ask questions before executing.

The workflow is always the same. Only the context changes.

Where Cowork Fails (Honestly)

⚡ **It burns through credits fast**

A single Cowork session can spend the equivalent of dozens of normal chat conversations. With the Pro plan ($20/month), you'll notice it in less than a week if you use it daily. If Cowork becomes your main workflow, consider the Max plan ($100/month).

🔬 **It's still a research version**

Anthropic states this explicitly. It can make mistakes. It can misinterpret files. Sometimes it picks a strange approach to a task when there's a simpler one available. Always review what it produces before sending it to a client.

💻 **It needs the app open**

If you close the Claude desktop app, the session dies. There's no mobile version. There's no web version. Cowork only works inside the desktop app on macOS or Windows.

🚫 **It's not for quick questions**

If you want to know "what's the capital of France", use Chat. Cowork is designed for multi-step tasks, not quick queries.

🤖 **Agents fail 10% of the time**

For complex tasks, Cowork divides the work into parts and executes them in parallel using multiple agents. Most of the time it's fast and accurate. Sometimes an agent takes a strange direction and the final result has a section that doesn't fit with the rest. Keep it on the radar.
