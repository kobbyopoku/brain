For the past few days, X has been full of discussion about Kimi K3. I want to offer a counterintuitive read: Kimi is not the real enemy of OpenAI and Anthropic. Neither is DeepSeek, and neither are the other open-weight models. The opponents that actually matter are the super platforms: Google, Meta, and Microsoft.

This is not to say open-weight models pose no threat. They are destroying one of the most important assets the frontier labs have: scarcity. But the competition between OpenAI, Anthropic, and the open-weight players is still, at its core, ordinary commercial competition. Everyone pays real training and inference costs. Everyone needs users, revenue, and capital. Everyone answers to the same economics.

Google, Meta, and Microsoft are fighting a different war. They do not need AI to be independently profitable. As long as AI protects search, advertising, cloud, productivity software, social platforms, and operating-system entry points, they can afford to subsidize it for far longer with profits from their existing businesses.

That is what makes this war brutal. It is not model versus model. It is this:

## Companies that must make money from AI are competing against companies that do not need AI to make money.

The win conditions were asymmetric from day one.

# What open weights destroy is not OpenAI. It is scarcity.

For years, the frontier-model business rested on one assumption: training a near-frontier model requires talent, data, capital, and infrastructure that only a handful of labs possess. As long as that capability stayed scarce, the leading labs could keep charging model rent.

Kimi, along with DeepSeek, Qwen, Llama, and the rest of the open-weight ecosystem, is dismantling that assumption. These models do not need to beat the best closed models every time. They do not need to beat them at all. Most enterprise tasks never required the strongest model in the world. What enterprises actually optimize for is return on spend at a given quality bar. A model with ninety percent of frontier capability at a fraction of the price is often the rational choice for support, translation, summarization, content generation, batch data work, and a good share of coding.

So the future enterprise will not run on one model. Complex engineering, serious research, and high-stakes decisions go to GPT or Claude. High-volume, standardized, low-risk work goes to cheaper open-weight or self-hosted models. What emerges is not a single-model monopoly but a routing layer that picks a model based on task value, complexity, latency, privacy, and the cost of failure. That routing layer pushes down API prices and erodes any margin built purely on model performance.

Open-weight companies cannot escape economics forever either. Weights can be free; training, inference, product, and infrastructure are not. If revenue never covers cost, capital constraints arrive eventually, open or closed.

Which is why the real strategic effect of open weights is not to destroy OpenAI and Anthropic directly. It is to prove that frontier models were never as scarce as everyone assumed.

## Once the scarcity premium collapses, the pricing logic of the entire industry collapses with it.

# The two sides are keeping different books

For OpenAI and Anthropic, every incremental use carries real inference cost. They live with a permanent set of questions: will users pay, can subscriptions cover heavy users, can API prices stay above compute cost, and once agents complete real tasks, can the platform take a cut of the transaction or the outcome.

Google, Meta, and Microsoft are not taking the same exam. Even if Gemini never turns a standalone profit, it pays for itself the moment it defends the search box and pulls Cloud and Workspace along. Even if Meta AI never sells a subscription, it pays for itself by improving recommendations, extending session time, lifting ad conversion, and cutting the cost of producing creative. Microsoft does not need any single model to justify itself, as long as AI grows Azure consumption and deepens the lock-in of Office and GitHub.

That is the fundamental asymmetry. AI-native companies must prove that AI itself generates revenue. Super platforms only need to prove that AI protects or strengthens the businesses they already own.

The platforms are not infinitely rich, to be clear. Alphabet raised its 2026 capital-expenditure guidance to 180 to 190 billion dollars in the first quarter. Meta raised its own to 125 to 145 billion. And the strain shows: in Q1 2026, Meta generated 32 billion dollars in operating cash flow, but after nearly 20 billion of capex, free cash flow was down to about 12 billion.

## The platforms are bleeding too. But platforms burn profits. AI startups burn funding. That is the real asymmetry in how long each side can stay in the war.

# Google has to win this one

Whether large models will transform social is still unclear. That they are transforming search is not. And search is not just one product among many at Google. It is the front door and the core cash engine of the entire empire.

Traditional search works like this: the user types keywords, Google returns links, the user clicks, and advertisers bid around those clicks and impressions. The AI loop is shorter: the user expresses an intent, the AI answers directly, and increasingly it just completes the task. If users stop clicking through and stay inside the generated answer, the entire economics of search advertising, its formats, inventory, click structure, and traffic allocation, comes under pressure.

So Google must push AI search, and the defense is not free. Google's real risk is not a lack of distribution. It is that every more complete AI answer protects the entry point while diluting the economics of the ads that entry point exists to sell. Google is trading a measure of self-cannibalization for the guarantee that the front door stays its own. That is its innovator's dilemma in one sentence:

## leave search alone and risk losing it to ChatGPT, or change search and erode its most mature business model.

And yet Google has no choice. It will not tolerate the starting point of human questions, research, and intent migrating at scale to someone else's product. It can embed Gemini into Search, Chrome, Android, Gmail, YouTube, Workspace, and Google Cloud. No new app to install, no data to migrate, no habit to build.

So far, the numbers say the defense is holding. On the Q1 2026 earnings call, Pichai reported search and other advertising revenue up 19 percent, search queries at an all-time high, Cloud up 63 percent and past 20 billion dollars in a quarter for the first time, and total paid subscriptions at 350 million. He also noted that after upgrading AI Overviews and AI Mode to Gemini 3, the cost of core AI responses fell by more than 30 percent. Google does not just own the distribution. Through TPUs, its own data centers, and engineering, it keeps driving down the cost of serving intelligence.

# Meta does not even need a new front door

Meta and Google both have enormous cash flow and enormous distribution, but their positions differ. Google is defending search. Meta gets to inject AI directly into its existing business model.

Meta's revenue is advertising. If AI improves recommendations, extends time spent, lifts conversion, generates creative automatically, lowers the barrier for merchants to advertise, and helps them find customers, the investment pays for itself inside the ad machine. In Q1 2026, Meta reported 56.3 billion dollars in revenue, up 33 percent year over year, with roughly 55 billion of it from ads.

Meta does not need to sell an API or a subscription. It can put AI inside WhatsApp, Instagram, Facebook, Messenger, and its smart glasses. That gives it something OpenAI and Anthropic can barely replicate: the ability to push AI to billions of existing users at close to zero incremental acquisition cost.

The deeper advantage is context. A user's social graph, content interests, creator follows, group chats, and commercial activity on Meta's platforms are context in themselves. What ChatGPT knows is mostly what users have chosen to hand it. What Meta may know is who you follow, what you like, who you talk to, what you are about to buy, and what your social circle is discussing right now. In the long war over the personal assistant, that context may matter more than model parameters.

Meta's biggest variable is equally clear: whether its own models ever get good enough. Distribution can amplify a competent product. It cannot save a visibly inferior one. If Meta's model capability, reliability, and product experience lag for years, billions of entry points will not convert into high-value AI usage on their own. The advantage is enormous. The victory is not automatic.

# Microsoft the most conflicted player on the board

If Google and Meta are the obvious super platforms, Microsoft is the most complicated one. It owns Windows, Office, GitHub, Azure, and a vast enterprise sales machine, while maintaining the deepest partnership in the industry with OpenAI. It is OpenAI's investor, infrastructure partner, and distribution channel, and at the same time it is building Copilot, its own models, and a complete enterprise AI platform.

In the short run the two grow together: OpenAI supplies frontier models and a consumer brand, Microsoft supplies cloud, enterprise distribution, and capital. In the long run, Microsoft will not settle for being OpenAI's compute vendor. What it wants is the entire enterprise AI stack, from infrastructure and models to productivity software, developer tools, data permissions, security, and the customer relationship itself. As value migrates from model capability toward entry points, agents, and enterprise workflows, the boundary between the two companies gets blurrier. Microsoft is simultaneously OpenAI's most important ally and the company best positioned to cap its platform ambitions.

And the ground the platforms are defending most fiercely is exactly the ground the AI-native companies most want to take.

# What OpenAI actually wants is not to sell more tokens

Everything OpenAI does is, at bottom, an argument for why you should open ChatGPT first. It wants ChatGPT to stop being a question-answering product and become the unified entry point through which people reach the internet, use software, and get things done. In the future OpenAI is building toward, you do not open a search engine, a browser, a travel site, a delivery app, a code editor, and your company's software separately. You state your intent to ChatGPT, and it books the hotel, plans the trip, runs the program, builds and deploys the software, manages the project, buys the product, and calls the enterprise system.

Once ChatGPT is the first receiver of user intent, OpenAI is no longer selling model capability. It decides which services get called, which products get shown, which merchant receives the traffic, and what commission comes off the transaction, while also charging users for subscriptions, enterprises for services, and developers for the platform. Its product moves already point unmistakably in this direction: ChatGPT, Codex, the browser, and agent capabilities are being pulled into a single front door.

So the collision with Google is not incidental. Both companies are fighting for the same thing: the right to be the first layer after a human expresses intent. Search companies want you to start at the search box. OpenAI wants you to start at ChatGPT. That conflict is structurally irreconcilable, and it will not stay theoretical for long. Three years, maybe less, and its basic shape will be visible.

I often say you do not need to wait ten years and dig up a tree to know what it will become. You only need to watch the direction it is growing now. The direction OpenAI is growing tells you it was never going to remain a model vendor.

# Anthropic is fighting a different war

OpenAI and Anthropic get discussed as a pair, but their strategies diverge. OpenAI is fighting for the consumer entry point. Anthropic leans into enterprise, coding, and high-value knowledge work.

In February 2026, Anthropic disclosed that Claude Code had passed 2.5 billion dollars in annualized revenue, more than doubling since the start of the year, while company-wide annualized revenue stood at roughly 14 billion. By April, its company-wide run rate had surpassed 30 billion. Enterprises pay directly for developer productivity, security, compliance, reliability, and code quality. And once Claude is embedded in a company's codebase, permission systems, and workflows, switching models stops being an API swap and becomes a re-evaluation of the entire production system.

So Anthropic does not need to win the consumer front door. It can aim to be the professional infrastructure of enterprise AI. But at that layer it cannot route around Google and Microsoft either. Google has Cloud, Workspace, Gemini, and a deep enterprise base. Microsoft has Azure, Office, GitHub, Windows, and the largest enterprise sales machine in software. If Gemini or Microsoft's own models reach good enough in enterprise settings, those platforms can bundle AI straight into existing contracts, permission systems, and software suites. Anthropic has avoided the loudest consumer war. It has not avoided the platform and infrastructure war.

# Good enough, and everywhere

When the capability gap is large, users seek out the strongest product. As the gap narrows, the value of the default entry point rises fast.

Say ChatGPT or Claude is a 100 and Gemini is a 95. But Gemini is already inside the search engine, the browser, the inbox, the phone, the office suite, and the cloud platform people open every day. Most users will not switch products, re-upload their files, rebuild their context, and migrate their workflows for the last five points. What beats the leader is rarely comprehensive technical superiority. It is usually this:

## Good enough, and everywhere.

Google does not need to beat OpenAI and Anthropic on every benchmark. It needs Gemini to be good enough, placed inside products that users and enterprises already cannot leave. The same holds for Microsoft. For a company already running Azure, Office, Windows, and GitHub, a good-enough model embedded in existing permissions and contracts can be more attractive than a stronger model that requires separate procurement and fresh integration.

# Your supplier, your investor, and your channel are also your competitor

The model war is also an infrastructure war. Google has decades of TPUs, data centers, and networking. Meta is building data centers at massive scale and pushing its own silicon. OpenAI and Anthropic have some of the strongest research teams in the field, and still depend on multiple platforms and chipmakers to scale compute.

OpenAI's disclosed cloud portfolio spans Microsoft, Oracle, AWS, CoreWeave, and Google Cloud, with compute partners including Nvidia, AMD, Cerebras, AWS Trainium, and custom silicon co-developed with Broadcom. Anthropic's compute spans three chip lines, Amazon's Trainium, Google's TPUs, and Nvidia GPUs, and Claude is sold to enterprises through AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure.

Which produces a deeply awkward arrangement: your supplier, your investor, and your distribution channel are also your competitor. Google can sell compute to Anthropic while Gemini competes with Claude. Amazon can invest in Anthropic while selling every vendor's models on AWS. Microsoft can fund and distribute OpenAI while building its own models and Copilot. The independent labs grow on platform infrastructure, and the demand they generate helps those same platforms build out data centers, improve chips and inference systems, accumulate enterprise AI experience, and lock in customer relationships. The more successful the model companies become, the stronger the infrastructure platforms sometimes get.

# The three migrations the model companies cannot avoid

As open weights keep pushing prices down, OpenAI and Anthropic cannot live on token margins forever. Three migrations are forced on them.

First, from selling models to owning the entry point, accumulating users' conversations, files, memory, preferences, permissions, and long-running work state until usage becomes habit. Second, from answering questions to completing tasks, because once AI writes code, operates software, manages projects, buys products, and calls enterprise systems, value is measured by what got done, not by how many tokens got generated. Third, from prepaid usage to outcome-based revenue, taking a share of transactions, software calls, advertising, enterprise automation, and agent services.

Only after all three migrations do they escape the base-model price war.

# In the end, it is not about who is smartest

This industry is addicted to leaderboards. GPT leads today, Claude tomorrow, then Gemini or some open-weight model closes the gap. But a single model release is one town in a long campaign. Wars are won by logistics, not by moments of genius.

The eventual winner will probably not be whoever once held the strongest model. It will be whoever simultaneously holds the most stable cash flow, the lowest acquisition and distribution costs, the widest entry points, the deepest enterprise workflows, the most independent infrastructure, the most complete business loop, and the longest tolerance for losses.

Seen this way, OpenAI and Anthropic occupy a strange position. They have the purest AI innovation capability and the earliest AI-native brands, and they lack what Google, Meta, and Microsoft have in abundance: mature cash engines, default entry points, and control of infrastructure. They have to sprint and build their business model, their infrastructure, and their entry points at the same time. The platforms get to generate cash from their existing empires while waiting for their own models to become good enough.

Which is why the real melee has probably not started. When Gemini and Meta AI are good enough and free inside products billions of people already use daily, how many users will keep opening ChatGPT and paying for it? When enterprises already run Google Cloud, Workspace, Azure, Office, and GitHub, how much appetite do they have to procure a standalone model company's full product? Meanwhile the open-weight ecosystem, global compute markets, energy, and chip manufacturing keep lowering the bar for frontier models to be replicated and commoditized.

The final question is not whether Kimi can beat GPT or Claude on some benchmark. The final question is this:

## When models themselves are no longer scarce, who still controls the entry points, the infrastructure, the workflows, and the cash flow?

That is what actually decides this war.
