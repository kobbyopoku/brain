## Phase 1: Build Your Toolkit (Days 1 to 14)

Before you sell anything, you have to be genuinely good at building. That means four skills.

**Skill 1: Context File Design**

Every automation starts with context. You need to interview a client, understand their workflow, and turn it into a context file that makes Claude produce industry-specific, voice-matched output.

Practice: build 3 context files for 3 hypothetical businesses in 3 different industries. A real estate brokerage. A marketing consultancy. A DTC e-commerce brand.

Each one should contain business description, audience, voice and tone, current projects, and quality bar.

**Skill 2: Sub-Agent Architecture**

Sub-agents are the engine. You need to design specialized agents that each do one job well.

Practice: build 5 sub-agents from scratch. A code reviewer. A weekly report generator. A cold email writer. A meeting notes cleaner. A bug reproducer.

Test each one 10 times with different inputs. Refine until they are reliable.

If you are not sure where to start, the agents in my "30 Claude Code Sub-Agents I Actually Use" article are paste-ready templates. Copy the YAML. Adapt the prompts. Ship in an hour.

**Skill 3: MCP Server Setup**

Connecting Claude to external tools is what separates a chatbot from a system. You need to be comfortable wiring up Tavily for web search, Google Drive for files, Gmail for email, Slack for comms, and at least one CRM.

Practice: set up all five MCP servers on your machine. Build one workflow that chains all of them. Search the web, save a summary to Drive, email it to yourself, post a notification in Slack, log it to your CRM.

If you can ship that one workflow end to end, you can sell.

**Skill 4: Scheduled Automation Design**

The highest-value automations run without anyone pressing a button.

Daily briefings. Weekly client reports. Monthly invoice processing. You need to understand /schedule, timing edge cases, and how to design automations that fail gracefully when data is missing.

Practice: build 3 scheduled automations on your own workflow. A daily briefing at 8am. A weekly review at 5pm Friday. A monthly summary on the 1st.

Run them for two weeks. Fix every break.

## Phase 2: Pick One Niche (Days 14 to 21)

Do not try to serve every business. Pick one niche and become the AI automation specialist for that industry.

**How to Pick**

You understand their workflow well enough to know what to automate.

They have enough revenue to pay $3,000 to $5,000 for a project.

They have at least 5 recurring tasks that follow a process.

They are not so technical that they would just build it themselves.

**Niches That Are Working Right Now**

**Real estate agencies**: CMA reports, listing descriptions, follow-up emails, market briefs.

**Marketing agencies**: Client reporting, content production, competitive teardowns, SEO briefs.

**E-commerce brands**: Product descriptions, review responses, inventory reports, support email.

**Professional services firms**: Proposals, SOPs, client onboarding, billing narratives.

**Content creators**: Research, drafting, repurposing, newsletter production.

**Financial advisors**: Client reports, market summaries, meeting prep, compliance docs.

**Healthcare practices**: Intake summaries, prior authorization drafts, patient comms.

**Law firms**: Document review, deposition summaries, client intake, billing letters.

Each niche has 5 to 10 high-value automations waiting to be built.

Pick one. Do not pick two. Two niches means zero referrals.

## Phase 3: Build Your First Case Study (Days 21 to 30)

Find one person in your niche. Build their automation for free.

In exchange you get three things worth more than money. A working production system. A case study with real numbers. A testimonial from a real client.

**How to Find Your First Free Client**

Post in the niche-specific community. "I am building AI automations for \[niche\] and looking for one \[role\] to work with for free in exchange for a case study."

Ask your network. Someone you know works in your target niche or knows someone who does.

Cold outreach to 10 businesses with this exact line: "I will build one AI automation that saves you at least 5 hours per week. Free. All I need is 2 hours of your time and permission to use the results as a case study."

10 messages. You will get 1 yes. That is enough.

**What to Build for Them**

Start with their most painful recurring task. The one they complain about every week.

Build the sub-agent. Set the context file. Connect the necessary MCP servers. Run it on real data, not toy data.

**Measure everything.** How long did the task take manually. How long does it take now. What is the quality difference. What is the weekly hours saved.

A case study that says "saved 8 hours per week on client reporting" with a real testimonial is worth $10,000 in marketing.

You now have proof.

## Phase 4: Package and Price (Days 30 to 35)

You have a working build. You have a case study. Time to productize.

**The Build Package**

This is what you deliver for a flat fee:

Discovery session of 2 hours to map their workflow.

Custom sub-agents for 3 to 5 automated tasks.

MCP server setup and configuration.

Context file tailored to their business.

Two weeks of testing and refinement.

Documentation so they can maintain it themselves.

30 days of support after launch.

**The Pricing Frame**

Price on value delivered, not hours worked.

If your build saves 10 hours per week at a $75/hour internal rate, that is $3,000/month in value. Forever.

Charging $3,000 to $5,000 one time for the build is a no-brainer ROI. The client pays once and saves every month for the life of the system.

For complex builds with multi-agent systems, custom MCP servers, or cross-platform integrations, charge $5,000 to $15,000.

**Never charge by the hour.** It punishes you for being fast.

## Phase 5: Find Paying Clients (Days 35 to 60)

You have the skills. You have the case study. You have the package. Now you need clients.

Three channels work.

**Channel 1: Content Marketing**

Post about what you build. Show before and after. Share specific time savings. Demonstrate the actual output.

Example: "I built an automation for a marketing agency that generates client reports in 3 minutes instead of 3 hours. Here is exactly how it works."

That post attracts the right audience. People who need the same thing.

One post per week. Four months in, you will have inbound.

**Channel 2: Direct Outreach**

Find businesses in your niche with obvious automation gaps. Send a short, specific message.

Example: "I built an AI system for \[similar business\] that automates \[specific task\] and saves them \[specific hours\] per week. Open to a 15-minute call to see if something similar would work for your workflow?"

3 to 5 outreach messages per day. 5 days a week. Within 30 days you will have paying clients.

The math: 100 outreach messages produces 10 calls produces 2 to 3 closes. That is your funnel.

**Channel 3: Referrals**

Your free case study client knows other people in their industry. Ask for introductions.

"Do you know 2 to 3 other \[roles\] who spend too much time on \[task\]? I would love to help them the same way I helped you."

One great referral is worth 10 cold emails. Referrals close at 50 to 70 percent.

## Phase 6: Scale to $10K Per Month (Days 60 to 90)

Once you have 3 to 5 paying clients, patterns emerge.

Every marketing agency needs the same 3 automations. Every e-commerce brand has the same 5 pain points. Every law firm complains about the same 4 document types.

That is your moat.

**Productize the Patterns**

Build template packages for each common use case in your niche.

Discovery sessions get shorter because you already know the workflow.

Builds get faster because you are customizing templates, not starting from zero.

Margins go up. Delivery time goes down. Client outcomes get better because you have already debugged the pattern with 5 previous clients.

**Add Recurring Revenue**

Offer monthly maintenance and optimization for $500 to $1,000 per month.

You refine their automations. You add new ones as needs change. You make sure everything keeps running when models update.

This converts project income into predictable monthly revenue.

**The Math**

5 clients on maintenance at $750 per month = $3,750 recurring.

2 new builds per month at $5,000 each = $10,000 project revenue.

**That is $13,750 per month. $165,000 per year. From a service that did not exist 18 months ago.**

The client list does not have to be huge. The work has to be excellent.

## The Tools You Actually Need

You can run this whole business with five tools.

**Claude Code or Cowork**: where the sub-agents live and run.

**Notion or Obsidian**: where you store context files, client SOPs, and case studies.

[Cal.com](https://cal.com/): for booking discovery calls without back-and-forth email.

**Stripe**: for invoicing. Use payment links so clients pay before you build.

**Loom**: for async client updates. One Loom replaces a 30-minute meeting.

That is the entire stack. Total cost under $200 per month.

## What Will Actually Stop You

Three things kill 90 percent of people who try this.

**Perfectionism on the toolkit.** They spend 8 weeks studying instead of building. Stop. Two weeks of practice is enough. The first paid client teaches you more than two months of theory.

**Pricing too low.** They charge $500 because they "are still learning." That signal kills the deal. Clients trust higher prices because higher prices signal expertise. Charge $3,000 minimum.

**No niche.** They want to serve every business. They build for nobody. Pick one industry. Get good. Then expand.

If you avoid these three traps, the rest is just reps.
