There's a thread on r/ProductManagement where a PM with kids asks whether it's okay if he skips the AI wave for a while.

I don't read him as anti-AI. He sounds like someone who has been running on empty. Every week brings another agent, another tip, another "AI PM salary" post, another "comment FREE" PDF.

A lot of commenters say the same thing. They are fine not using AI for thinking, PRDs, or specs. What helps them is the dull work: write a query, run it, drop the result in a sheet, stop pinging engineering for table joins.

Other commenters took a different route and stopped following the LinkedIn stream. They want domain depth more than a thousand shallow AI tips.

At some companies, saying "I'm not using AI" reads like a soft layoff signal, because leaders want more output and they are watching.

At others, leaders barely understand the tools, so talking about AI is enough and the workflows never change. Both setups create the same mess: people stuck performing AI theater without changing how work gets done.

Someone asked whether "AI PM" is a real skill, or the 2000s Agile interview trick in a new outfit. Back then, knowing standup and Jira could get you hired.

## What I would tell their leaders

Stop requiring AI theater. If your review updates are full of tool talk rather than shipped work, the process is rewarding theater.

I don't think every PM needs to keep up with the content firehose. Most of it is marketing, so the signal-to-noise ratio stays low. Give them a short filter: a few trusted sources, and permission to ignore the rest.

Plug the tools into the context: data location, docs, tickets, and review standards. A chat window with no company context produces confident garbage, and then you become the person who has to check every draft.

Keep product AI and personal productivity separate. Building reliable LLM features in the product is a craft. Using Claude to rewrite a PRD is not. When you mix those two, people feel behind for the wrong reasons. They see every new agent pattern as a career requirement.

## Where a shared method helps

Think of it as the files and routines that stop every PM inventing their own prompt folder, quality bar, and review ritual. That is different from another LinkedIn study list.

When leadership wants AI adoption without becoming the editor of every draft, they need shared workflows, shared review standards, and enough product context that the model is not guessing. That is the boring infrastructure version of this wave. Boring is good right now.

Here's how a leader can test this on Monday: pick one live workflow, write down the review bar for "good enough," and make two curious PMs run that workflow with the same context files for a week. Then compare the drafts to see where quality diverges. Uneven quality points to a missing shared method: the review bar, context files, or workflow steps.

You can sit out the hype. Choosing not to adopt a shared way of working is a different bet.
