# Abstraction-Altitude Framing

> The observation that the level of abstraction at which a question is posed determines the level at which it is discussed — and that high-altitude questions let everyone sound smart while avoiding hard, specific thinking.

## Definition

Per [[wiki/sources/shreyas-get-to-the-core-of-the-thing]], **abstraction-altitude framing** is the principle that *the framing decides the discussion*: people rise to whatever level of abstraction the question opens up. A high-altitude question ("should we go wide or deep?") produces a high-altitude conversation in which everyone can contribute confidently and nobody has to do the hard, specific thinking. The prescribed remedy is to deliberately **drop the question "one level down"** — re-pose it at a lower altitude where answers require concrete knowledge and specific commitments.

## Why it matters

This is the meta-cognitive mechanism beneath [[product-strategy]] (and beyond it — it generalizes to any decision conversation). It names *why* strategy meetings drift toward comfortable abstraction and gives a concrete lever to counter it. It is the human-strategy sibling of [[question-form-first]]: both rest on the kernel that the form of the question is load-bearing, but one operates in human decision-making and the other in agent-UX intake.

## Open questions and contradictions

- Single source so far. Unresolved: at what altitude is a given question *correctly* posed? Dropping a level is not always right — some questions (capital allocation, mission) belong at high altitude. The essay does not specify a stopping rule.
