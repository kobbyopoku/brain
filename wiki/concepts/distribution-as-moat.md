# Distribution as the moat

> The thesis that, once AI has collapsed the cost and difficulty of building software, competitive advantage no longer lives in the product but in distribution — the audience and attention that determine whether anyone knows the product exists.

## Definition

**Distribution as the moat** is the argument that AI has obliterated the technical / build moat, so the durable asymmetry has shifted to **distribution**: an existing audience and the attention it represents. In a world where building is cheap and fast, the scarce, defensible asset is being known. The maxim from [[wiki/sources/exploraX_-5-solo-ai-business-models]] states it directly: *"AI changed what you build. It didn't change the fact that someone has to know you exist before they pay you."*

## Why it matters

This concept is the unifying thesis across two otherwise-different sources in this wiki, and it reframes where Godwin should invest effort. If the build is no longer the moat, then for any of the solo-AI business models — [[ai-engineer]], [[micro-saas]], [[selling-websites]] — the marginal hour is better spent on audience-building than on incremental product polish. It also explains acquisition behavior: an acquirer may buy a vibecoded app not for its code but for the attention it has accumulated, which they could not build themselves.

## Treatment across sources

- [[wiki/sources/exploraX_-5-solo-ai-business-models]] frames it as the **unifying thesis**: all five solo-AI business models share one hidden dependency — distribution. *"AI changed what you build. It didn't change the fact that someone has to know you exist before they pay you."* Winners are consistent audience-builders, not the best-skilled.
- [[wiki/sources/mattgittleson-vibecoded-b2c-375k-exit]] frames it as the **core thesis of the exit**: AI has obliterated the technical/build moat, so distribution is where the asymmetry now lives. Acquirers buy *"attention they couldn't build themselves,"* not the app. This source anchors the concept alongside the marketing/CRO cluster.

## Sub-claims and details

- **The build moat is gone**: AI has collapsed the cost and difficulty of building, so the product itself no longer differentiates ([[wiki/sources/exploraX_-5-solo-ai-business-models]], [[wiki/sources/mattgittleson-vibecoded-b2c-375k-exit]]).
- **Distribution is the hidden dependency**: all five solo-AI business models depend on it whether or not they name it ([[wiki/sources/exploraX_-5-solo-ai-business-models]]).
- **Winners are audience-builders, not the best-skilled**: consistency of audience-building beats skill ([[wiki/sources/exploraX_-5-solo-ai-business-models]]).
- **Acquirers buy attention, not code**: a vibecoded app's value at exit is the attention it represents — *"attention they couldn't build themselves"* ([[wiki/sources/mattgittleson-vibecoded-b2c-375k-exit]]).

## Open questions and contradictions

- Both sources assert the build moat is "gone" or "obliterated," but neither addresses domains where build difficulty remains a moat (regulated, high-reliability, or deeply integrated systems — e.g. [[wiki/projects/vedge|Vedge]]). The thesis is strongest for low-complexity B2C and small-business software; its limits in harder domains are unexamined.
- The two sources agree distribution is the asymmetry but differ in scope: one treats it as a forward strategy for building a solo business, the other as the retrospective explanation for an acquisition. Whether the same audience-building tactics serve both is unresolved.
