# Online Business Models — 2026

> Ten online business models that work in 2026 (per Brian Moran's SamCart-data-informed catalog) plus one that's collapsing. Recurring thesis: as AI commoditizes information, the human-touch premium grows; recurring billing only survives when the product is used daily; physical goods and high-ticket coaching are the safest hedges.

## Definition

A snapshot of which online business models continue to monetize in 2026 given AI-driven commoditization of information. Each model is characterized by price point, delivery shape, and what makes it AI-resistant or AI-vulnerable.

## Why it matters

This concept is the wiki's go-to reference when a new source argues for or against a specific monetization shape. Every recurring-revenue source can be checked against the "daily use only" rule; every AI-services source can be checked against the "AI services for businesses" framing.

The catalog also functions as a **falsifiability list**: if a future source claims, say, that monthly $50 communities are thriving, that's a real disagreement with the canonical source here that should be captured under "Treatment across sources."

## Treatment across sources

- [[wiki/sources/realbrianmoran-making-money-online-2026]] — canonical wiki source. Catalog of 10 working models + 1 dying. Author bases the catalog on top-seller data from [[wiki/entities/samcart]] (claimed $6B+ in processed sales for 100k+ entrepreneurs). Master thesis: *"Pure information is becoming free... the human touch is becoming MORE valuable as AI takes over information."*

## The catalog

### What's collapsing
- **Monthly $20-$200 communities.** Churn destroys them. Most members pay for something they don't use even once a month.

### 1. High-ticket coaching ($2K-$10K+)
- Where the real money is, and getting more valuable.
- Premise: AI gives generic advice; you give specific judgment about a specific person's specific situation.

### 2. Low-ticket digital products ($7-$97)
- Not the business — the **customer acquisition layer**.
- A customer is worth 10× a lead.
- The mistake: thinking the low-ticket product **is** the business.

### 3. Newsletters with yearly billing (~$100/year)
- Yearly removes the monthly cancel-decision.
- "$100/year for valuable content" is an impulse buy that survives.
- [[wiki/entities/agora]] is the canonical example.

### 4. Supplements and physical products
- AI-resistant.
- Top sellers on SamCart right now.
- The hedge: *"You could wake up 12 to 18 months from now, and your course sales are gone."*

### 5. Build your own apps via AI
- [[wiki/entities/claude-code]], [[wiki/entities/cursor]], [[wiki/entities/replit]].
- Replace static digital products with apps you couldn't have built before.
- Author claims he built an iOS app in an hour.
- Cross-cuts with [[ai-automation-services]] and [[services-as-software]].

### 6. In-person events ($2K-$5K tickets)
- High-touch, AI-resistant.
- Compounds the brand: testimonials, content, superfans.

### 7. Exclusive high-ticket communities ($5K+/year)
- *"Get 50 members at $5,000/year"* — not 5,000 at $50/month.
- Half the reason people buy is identity / status / belonging.

### 8. Software and tools — daily use only
- The only recurring model the author would start.
- *"Do people use it every day? If yes, recurring works. If not, don't even try."*

### 9. Investing / financial advice
- $2K-$5K access offers for newsletters and portfolios.
- *"People will always pay for help with their money."*

### 10. AI services for businesses ($5K-$20K/project)
- *"Demand is insane."*
- See [[ai-automation-services]] for the deeper treatment and [[services-as-software]] for the scaled version.

### What to avoid
- Monthly communities at $10-$200/month.
- Info products AI can replace.
- Anything where people don't use it daily but pay monthly.
- Get-rich-quick schemes (always a no).

## Sub-claims and details

### The recurring four-part pattern

The source distills the catalog into four rules:
1. **Human touch is becoming MORE valuable, not less** — charge for experience, judgment, presence.
2. **Low-ticket front + high-ticket back** still works — acquire customers cheaply, sell premium transformation.
3. **Recurring only works if people use it daily.**
4. **Physical products are massively underrated.**

### Cross-source resonance

- The source's model #10 (AI services) is the same business model [[ai-automation-services]] and [[services-as-software]] cover in operational depth.
- Models #1, #6, #7 (high-touch, in-person, exclusive) all key on AI-resistant human delivery — a single underlying thesis.
- Models #5 (build your own apps) and #10 (AI services) both depend on the [[claude-code-skills]] / [[mcp-server]] / [[agentic-loop]] stack covered elsewhere in the wiki.

### The 12-18-month claim

The source asserts course sellers are 12-18 months from being undercut by AI. The author's recommendation: diversify into physical, coaching, events, or apps before forced.

## Open questions and contradictions

- **The "monthly communities are dying" claim** contradicts a popular thread in 2025-2026 creator-economy content. If a future source argues for monthly communities, this disagreement gets logged here under "Treatment across sources."
- **SamCart-data bias**: the author's catalog is informed by what sells on SamCart specifically. SamCart's audience may not represent the broader online-business landscape.
- **Author's claim "$6B+ in processed sales for 100k+ entrepreneurs"** is unverified; treat as the author's prior.
- **The "build your own app" framing assumes** the user can describe what to build, which is a real bottleneck the source elides.
