# Product Strategy

> The set of specific bets a product team makes on specific features, derived from deep customer knowledge — not the choice between abstract strategic binaries.

## Definition

Per [[wiki/sources/shreyas-get-to-the-core-of-the-thing]], **product strategy** is not the act of choosing between high-level binaries (e.g. "go wide or go deep," "horizontal or vertical"). Those abstractions are where strategy conversations *fail*. Real product strategy is the concrete set of [[specific-bets]] on specific features, grounded in [[customer-insight]] and aimed at defensible [[product-differentiation]]. The strategic shape of a product (wide vs deep, etc.) is an *output* derived from those bets, not a choice made in the abstract.

## Why it matters

The concept earns wiki-shelf-space because it is directly load-bearing for [[wiki/projects/vedge|Vedge]], [[wiki/projects/kivora|Kivora]], and [[wiki/projects/clarvyn|Clarvyn]] — three commercial products where Godwin owns the strategy. Doshi's argument is a guardrail against a common failure mode: leadership conversations that operate at a comfortable altitude where everyone sounds smart while the hard, specific work goes undone.

## Treatment across sources

- [[wiki/sources/shreyas-get-to-the-core-of-the-thing]] frames product strategy as the core domain of the essay: strategy conversations fail at high altitude; real strategy is specific bets on specific features, not the choice between abstract binaries.

## Sub-claims and details

- The choice between strategic binaries (wide/deep, horizontal/vertical) is a *distraction*, not the strategy itself. [[wiki/sources/shreyas-get-to-the-core-of-the-thing]]
- Real differentiating bets come from deep knowledge of what specific customers need. [[wiki/sources/shreyas-get-to-the-core-of-the-thing]]
- The hard strategic questions are: can you conceive real [[product-differentiation]] against stiff competition, and can you build it creatively and quickly? [[wiki/sources/shreyas-get-to-the-core-of-the-thing]]

## Open questions and contradictions

- The essay is a single source; the wiki has not yet ingested a counter-position arguing that high-altitude framing is sometimes the correct level (e.g. for capital allocation or portfolio decisions).
