# How I Quit Sam Altman and Built a $7M Services-as-Software Agency — Alex Vacca

> A founder's playbook from a $7M ARR / 70-client / 30-person bootstrapped services-as-software agency ([[wiki/entities/coldiq|ColdIQ]]). Coins (or popularizes) the term [[services-as-software]] — agency-shape on the contract, software-margin underneath via a content-driven accelerator layer. The most operationally substantive source on AI-services-business economics ingested so far.

## TL;DR

After two years at [[wiki/entities/worldcoin|Worldcoin]] (Sam Altman's iris-scanning company), Alex Vacca quit corporate to build [[wiki/entities/coldiq|ColdIQ]] with co-founder Mich. Four years in: $7M ARR, 70 clients, 30+ people across 10 countries, bootstrapped, no VC. The article packages the four playbooks behind that: (1) landing first clients with risk reversal + content, (2) the first-three-hires sequence, (3) churn defense at scale (with explicit math: 70 clients × 10% monthly churn = 7 replacements/month, a full-time closer's quota), (4) documentation discipline so the business survives without any single person. The closing distinction — *"the contract still says services; the infrastructure underneath runs like software"* — defines [[services-as-software]] as a sibling to [[ai-automation-services]] with explicit software-margin mechanics.

## Key takeaways

### Origin and scale
- Author hit a corporate ceiling at [[wiki/entities/worldcoin|Worldcoin]] (€80k salary, 4% raise ceiling, manager-credit-takes-credit) and decided to never let someone else cap his growth.
- Joined his friend Mich's existing agency; first client was via a friend Nicholas's recruitment business.
- Four years later: $7M+ ARR, 70 clients, 30+ people, 10 countries, bootstrapped (no VC, no angel, no pitch deck).

### Playbook 1: Land clients with zero proof
Two fronts simultaneously, both required:
- **Front 1: Risk reversal.** Anchor every guarantee to something measurable ("if we don't hit X by date Y, you pay nothing"); use free deliverables (lead lists, audits, sample workflows) as conversation starters; time-box every guarantee (30 or 60 days); refuse to compete on price.
- **Front 2: Build trust in public.** Pick one platform (LinkedIn for them) and post daily for 6 months minimum; share the actual workflow with frameworks and screenshots; plan for the viral post (Mich shipped one that produced their first inbound wave); make sure when prospects Google your name, they don't land on nothing.
- **Combined effect**: risk reversal alone gets you a meeting; daily content alone gets a follower; stacked, they produce a paying client who arrives already half-sold.

### Playbook 2: First-three-hires sequence
- **Only A players** — people who own a domain end-to-end without micromanagement. No "nice to have" hires. Hire slowly when growth screams to hire fast.
- **Hire 1: Delivery operator** owning multiple client accounts after a short shadow.
- **Hire 2: Second delivery operator** of same caliber. Two operators force you to write down what you do (no more tribal knowledge).
- **Hire 3: Delivery lead** managing the first two and owning quality.
- Hold off on marketer / AE / head of ops until the delivery layer runs without you.

### Playbook 3: Churn defense at scale (the math)
- 10 clients × 10% monthly churn = 1 replacement/month.
- 30 clients = 3 replacements/month.
- 70 clients (where ColdIQ is) = **7 replacements/month — a full-time closer at quota who contributes zero to net new growth.**
- *"You cannot out-sell churn at scale."* Three fronts:
  - **Front 1**: Promise only what you're 99% sure you can deliver. Re-anchor as under-promise. Track promise-vs-delivery monthly per account.
  - **Front 2**: Own the meeting cadence; never lose control of the room. Walk in with the next plan; fixed weekly/bi-weekly cadence; treat speed as the deliverable; written recap within 24 hrs.
  - **Front 3**: Add a revenue layer that doesn't depend on more delivery headcount. (Their **cold accelerator** is content-driven; margin compounds at the content layer; covers churn-bad-month volatility.)

### Playbook 4: Documentation discipline
- **Workflow files in Markdown** — one file per repeatable task (onboarding, deliverability audit, billing escalation), written for someone who started Monday with zero context. (This is [[markdown-as-agent-contract]] applied to ops.)
- **Loom videos** for visual work (Cursor-driven workflows, dashboard navigation).
- **Decision logs per client** — every "why did we do it this way" note, dated.
- **Escalation protocols** for failure modes (deliverability tank, missed kickoff, A-player resignation).
- Once written, automate the no-human-needed parts. *"With Claude Code now, internal tools that used to cost tens of thousands of dollars to build are weekend projects."*

### The services-as-software thesis
- *"The contract still says services. The infrastructure underneath runs like software."*
- The accelerator (a content-margin layer on top of services delivery) is what makes the math work. Margin compounds at the content layer; that compounding carries the business across services cyclicality.
- *"Pricing pressure on hour-based work is going to compress every operator who hasn't built infrastructure underneath the human layer. Tasks an LLM can do for twenty dollars are going to get priced like that."*
- What survives the LLM compression: judgment, relationship, taste, accountability.

### The 12-week program
- Author packaged the playbook into a 12-week program at `aiagency.io` — same operating system ColdIQ runs internally, including 11 internal-stack AI agents. Built off 239 client-analyses and 2,216 campaigns. 155 agencies have launched on this stack. *(Author's claim, not independently verified.)*
