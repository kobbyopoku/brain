# Shreyas Doshi — Get to the Core of the Thing

> A product-advisor essay arguing that high-altitude strategic binaries ("wide vs. deep", "CAC vs. LTV", "platform vs. point") are seductive theater that should be *refused*; the real product question always lives one level down, in specific bets on specific features grounded in specific customer insight.

## TL;DR

A founder of an AI startup, rattled by two incumbents entering her space, asks Shreyas Doshi whether to "go wide or go deep." His answer: that is the wrong question, and the job is to refuse the binary entirely. High-abstraction framings feel strategic and let everyone in the room "sound smart" without requiring real customer knowledge or creativity — but the truth is always one level down: *what specific feature will get this specific customer to buy and stay?* If you can articulate the specific differentiating bets, the wide/deep shape falls out automatically; if you can't, no framework or analogy will save you.

## Key takeaways

- **The framing decides the discussion.** People rise to whatever level of abstraction the question opens up; ask "wide or deep" in a room of smart people and you'll spend 40 minutes debating "wide or deep."
- **High-altitude binaries are theater.** "Wide vs. deep", "platform vs. point solution", "horizontal vs. vertical", "reduce CAC vs. increase LTV" are *universally seductive* because they confer social status and sound clever without requiring deep customer knowledge or creativity.
- **The real question is one level down — always.** Not "wide or deep" but "what is going to work? what feature is going to resonate? what will get this person — the one whose call you took yesterday — to actually buy and actually stay?"
- **This is product 101, and that's the point.** It is unsatisfying precisely because it strips away strategic-sounding language and forces you to stand in front of a harder question: *do you actually know what your customers need, can you conceive real differentiation against stiff competition, and can you build it creatively and quickly?*
- **If yes, the wide/deep debate is unnecessary.** The shape of the product (a little wide here, a little deep there) *follows from* the specific bets driven by customer insight and market understanding — not from an hour-long (or day-long offsite) abstraction debate.
- **If no, no framework saves you.** Going "deep" on the wrong thing because someone used a great analogy ("we want to be Lovable for the construction supply chain") is just a slow, painful way to be wrong.
- **Competitive anxiety triggers the wrong reflex.** The founder's instinct to debate surface-area expansion was a response to incumbent entry; Doshi redirects the energy from posture to specifics.
- **Evocative analogies are a tell, not a strategy.** Sports metaphors, podcast anecdotes, Stripe references, Kano-model citations, and "[famous product] for [niche]" framings substitute fluency for the hard thinking about specific features.
- **The discipline generalizes.** "Whenever someone hands you a binary at altitude dressed up in clever business-speak... your job is to refuse it. Get to the core of the thing. Then talk about that."
